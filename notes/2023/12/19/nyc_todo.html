Money

  • Stores, food establishments (even chains), bodegas, 7-11s, etc present their prices as pre-tax. New York City sales tax ranges from 4% to 8.875%, and some products are exempt from it.
  • Be prepared to tip everywhere. On restaurants it is customary to tip 20%. There is some debate about whether you should calculate the tip on the amount before or after tax.
  • You don’t really need cash. You do need it if you want to buy from street vendors, or if you want to dodge the additional cost that certain sellers inpose on card transations (like the shwarma above)
  • On restaurants and entertainment venues, it is common practice for the attendand to leave with the credit card, to later return with an attached invoice that is meant to be signed and filled with the tipping amount.
    • It also happened to me that when trying to pay by contactless card to a market vendor, the card kept being rejected, and since the card insertion feature was not available, the vendor wrote down the card number and details into the system to proceed with the payment. That failed as well, because the card got blocked by my bank due to a false-positive suspected fraud, as I later discovered. Eventually I just payed with cash to resolve the issue.
    • As an european, the practice of being physically separated from the card, verification via signature, and freely writting down credit card numbers strike me as quite unsecure.

Getting Around

  • Walking through the perpendicular numbered streets and avenues is an absolute bliss, and makes it quite easy to navigate through them. Several times I just looked at the map and quickly memorized something like “3 right, 2 left”, which translated to “cross 3 roads and turn right, and then cross 2 roads and turn left”. This would keep me walking going for a long time and have time to appreciate the surroundings, without the need to constantly check the map.
  • Subway and Bus now have OMNI, which just means that you can tap in with your credit / debit card. Each ride costs each ride is $2.90 everywhere, there are no different prices for different zones. This has a [rolling 7 day price cap] (https://omny.info/fares), so you never pay more than $34 over that period.
    • Metro Card is the pre-paid version of this. You need one, buy them directly from the machines to avoid being scammed. For example, a 7-Day Unlimited MetroCard costs $34 upfront, and can be used any number of times during those 7 days.
      • Be aware that if you swipe in, get out from the station, and then attempt to shortly after swipe in again, it will reject your swipe, due to being to recent. If that happens, ask a MTA attendant to let you in (if available), or do like many others and wait for someone to crack open the emergency exit from the inside. If you hear an alarm going off in the station, most likely is because someone opened the emergency exit to let other’s in.
  • Just like routine events such as figuring out post-tax prices purchases or how much and when to tip require some kind of mental effort, figuring out the different edge cases of the subway system will keep your mind busy. It is like there is a perpetual rat race game being played in the city.
    • Sometimes trains stop on all stations, sometimes they don’t, sometimes trains have indicators of which station you are in, sometimes they don’t, sometimes the station has (non-standard) indications of how much time it takes for next train to arrive, sometimes they don’t. Sometimes it rains heavily inside the stations, sometimes it doesn’t.

Closing Thoughts

Ever since I was a kid I had considrable fascination with the USA. The culture that arrived through the small and big screens, the big companies and entrewpnrurs that I admired, the language, the people I’ve met along the way, the stories. Having had the oporunity to visit it and expirence first hand was a true priviledge. I’ve lived it like it was the last time I would visit it, and it imprinted in me several thoughts and perspetives that I’m sure to last my lifetime.

New York is no Disney Land, and Elmo’s pictures in times square don’t come for free either, so set expectations accordingly. It can be one of the most fulfilling experiences you’ll ever have. I advise you to come prepared and plan accordingly.

Frank sinatra sang that he wants “to wake up in the city that doens’t sleep”. That phrase has a deep meaning, that only struck me upon my trip. The city does not indeed sleep. Times sqaure lights, 24 hour subway, the constant movement. Living in NYC does wakes you up, not only literally, but also figuratively. It’s the wake up towards a different reality that keeps you on your toes in several ways. In a way, it is a celebration of being alive.

It feels like you are constantly playing a game when living in New York
